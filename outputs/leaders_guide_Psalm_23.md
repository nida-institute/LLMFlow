# StoryFlow Leader's Guide: [Replace with specific Bible passage]

This guide helps you lead a StoryFlow Bible Study. StoryFlow works like watching a movie - we look at scenes from the Bible and go through these simple steps:

- **Step 1: Senses (What's Happening?)** Put yourself in the scene. What do you see, hear, and feel? Imagine being there as an observer. Try seeing it through different people's eyes. What would they notice?

- **Step 2: Context (What's the Background?)** Think about the world of the story. What was life like for these people? What happened before this scene? How does knowing the background help you understand what's going on?

- **Step 3: Spiritual and Emotional Journey (What's at the Heart for Them?)** Think about what the people in the story might be feeling and thinking. What are they struggling with? How are they growing?

- **Step 4: Connections (What's at the Heart for Us?)** How does this story connect to our own lives? What can we learn that matters to us today?

- **Step 5: Summary (What Do We Call It?)** Give this scene a simple title that captures its main idea. What few words best describe this part of the story?

## Guiding the Conversation

As the leader, your job is to help everyone talk and share together. Keep these notes to yourself - participants don't need handouts. The goal is for everyone to connect with each other, not with paper.

### Tips for Effective Leadership

- **Get to Know the Questions**: Spend time with the questions before your group meets. You don't need to memorize them, but understanding the questions and getting to know them helps you guide the conversation naturally and authentically.

- **Go With the Flow**: Every group is different. Feel free to skip questions that don't fit or add new ones that do. Follow where the conversation leads.

- **Build Community**: Help everyone explore the Bible passage together. Make sure everyone feels comfortable sharing their thoughts.

- **Be Present**: Focus on engaging with your group rather than following the guide perfectly. Your authentic presence and active listening create better discussions than perfect questions.

Using this guide will help you create a meaningful Bible study experience where everyone can participate. Enjoy discovering the story together with your group.

## The Lord is My Shepherd (Psalm 23:1)
*The Lord is my shepherd; I shall not want.*

### Leader’s Guide for Psalm 23:1

**Leader’s Transition:**
*"Let’s take a moment to step into this passage with our senses and imagination. Picture yourself in the world these words describe — what do you see, hear, and feel? Let’s slow down and experience the message together."*

**Sensory Engagement:**

1. **Visualize the Scene:**
   - "Imagine a tranquil landscape before you. As a shepherd guides the sheep, what do you see in this pastoral scene? Are there green fields, gentle hills, or a winding path? Who or what draws your attention?"
   
2. **Sound and Silence:**
   - "As you picture this scene, what sounds can you hear? Is there a gentle breeze rustling through the grass, the soft murmurs of the sheep, or perhaps the rhythmic step of the shepherd as he leads?"

3. **Emotional Landscape:**
   - "Consider the phrase, 'The Lord is my shepherd; I shall not want.' What emotions arise as you hear these words? Is there a sense of peace, security, or trust? How might it feel to be part of this scene?"

**Character and Emotion:**

1. **Perspective of the Psalmist:**
   - "Think about the speaker saying, 'The Lord is my shepherd; I shall not want.' What might it feel like to express such confidence and contentment? What might the world look like through the eyes of someone who trusts so deeply?"

2. **Presence of the Shepherd:**
   - "Imagine being the shepherd in this scene. What thoughts or feelings might accompany the responsibility of guiding and providing for the sheep? How might the shepherd view the landscape around him?"

3. **The Sheep’s Experience:**
   - "Imagine you are one of the sheep under the care of this shepherd. What senses or emotions arise in knowing that every need is being met? What details of this cared-for world stand out to you?"

**Rhythm and Movement:**

1. **Flow of the Verse:**
   - "Consider the movement in, 'The Lord is my shepherd; I shall not want.' What pace or rhythm do you notice in these words? Does the line feel steady, flowing, or grounded in some way?"

2. **Imagined Journey:**
   - "Picture the journey through this verse. How might the landscape change as you move through it? Are there any transitions or contrasts that catch your attention as you progress from the role of a shepherd to one who lacks nothing?"

By immersing in these sensory and emotional elements, participants are invited to step into the world of Psalm 23:1, experiencing its imagery and emotional depth first-hand.

### Exploring the Historical and Cultural Context of Psalm 23:1

Let's delve into the historical, cultural, and emotional backdrop of "The Lord is My Shepherd" from Psalm 23:1, using the Berean Standard Bible translation: "The Lord is my shepherd; I shall not want."

#### Highlight the Broader Setting

1. **Shepherding in Ancient Israel:**
   - Shepherding was a common occupation in ancient Israel, reflecting a pastoral society where sheep were a valuable resource for wool, meat, and sacrificial offerings. Shepherds were responsible for the constant care and protection of their flock against predators and harsh environments.
   - **Open-ended Question:** What might it have felt like to depend completely on a shepherd for safety and well-being, analogous to trusting in God for provision and guidance?

2. **Spiritual Symbolism of Shepherd:**
   - In the biblical context, the image of God as a shepherd underlines themes of guidance, provision, and care. It aligns with the idea of covenantal relationships, where God assumes the role of protector and provider.
   - **Open-ended Question:** How does envisioning God as a shepherd enhance your understanding of the relationship between the divine and humanity?

#### Cultural or Biblical Insights

3. **Honor/Shame Framework:**
   - In ancient Near Eastern cultures, the community's honor was often linked to the well-being of its members. A shepherd failing to care for their flock would bring shame, while a caring shepherd upheld communal honor.
   - **Open-ended Question:** How might viewing God as a shepherd change the community’s perception of divine honor and the responsibility toward one another?

4. **Poetry and Reception:**
   - Biblical poetry often used vivid imagery and metaphor to convey deep truths. The shepherd metaphor in this scene would have resonated with Israelites as both a literal and symbolic assurance of divine presence and provision.
   - **Open-ended Question:** What emotions or reflections does the metaphor of a shepherd evoke when considering one’s personal relationship with God?

#### Exegetical Lenses

5. **Covenantal Framework:**
   - The idea of God as a shepherd ties into the broader covenantal narrative, where God promises to lead and protect Israel, reinforcing themes of faithfulness and loyalty.
   - **Open-ended Question:** How does interpreting this relationship through a covenantal lens alter your understanding of what it means to "not want"?

By exploring these elements, we gain a fuller picture of the ancient world and the profound trust expressed in the words, "The Lord is my shepherd; I shall not want." This trust is deeply rooted in historical, cultural, and spiritual contexts that resonate throughout the ages.

## Exploring Psalm 23:5 – "A Table Prepared"

Let's delve into the **historical, cultural, and emotional context** of this passage to better understand its depth and significance.

### Shepherd Imagery
In ancient Israel, the image of a shepherd was deeply resonant. Shepherds were protectors and providers, guiding their flocks to safety and sustenance. When Psalm 23 speaks of God preparing a table, it evokes the careful and attentive provision of a shepherd for his sheep. This conveys not just physical provision but also protection amidst adversities.

- **Question:** How might imagining God as a shepherd, who prepares a table for you amidst danger, change how you view challenges or threats in your life?
- **Question:** What emotions arise when you consider the care and attention implied in this imagery?

### Anointing with Oil
Anointing with oil was a significant cultural practice in biblical times, symbolizing blessing, empowerment, and selection for a special purpose. It was often used in the context of hospitality, cleansing, and sanctification. In this verse, anointing could signify being chosen and honored by God despite the presence of adversaries.

- **Question:** How does the act of anointing change your perception of your own value or purpose in the face of difficulties?
- **Question:** Can you think of moments when you felt especially honored or set apart, even when surrounded by challenges?

### A Cup Overflows
The overflowing cup is a powerful symbol of abundance and blessing. In a culture where survival was often a daily concern, the image of a cup that overflows suggests divine generosity and provision beyond immediate needs. It speaks to God's ability to provide more than what is merely necessary or expected.

- **Question:** What does it mean to you to imagine abundance in areas of your life where scarcity or lack might typically dominate?
- **Question:** In what ways can you identify moments of “overflow” in your life, and how do they shape your perspective on provision and sufficiency?

### Presence of Enemies
The notion of being blessed ("a table prepared") in the presence of one's enemies challenges typical feelings of fear or vulnerability. In an honor/shame culture, public blessings in front of adversaries would serve to vindicate and elevate the recipient's status.

- **Question:** What does it mean for you to be affirmed and blessed in a context that includes opposition or enmity?
- **Question:** How does this scene redefine power and the notion of victory in your life?

### Conclusion
Understanding the cultural and historical backdrop of Psalm 23:5 allows us to appreciate the depth of God's care, provision, and empowerment. These insights offer a lens not only to see the text's richness but also to reflect on its application in our lives today. What further insights do you gain when considering this passage's promise of provision and blessing amidst adversities?

### Stepping into the World of Psalm 23:6

**Broader Setting:**
Psalm 23 is traditionally attributed to King David, the shepherd-turned-king of Israel. It's a psalm of trust and comfort, written in the context of David's experience with God as a shepherd cares for his sheep. This verse is part of a poetic expression of faith in divine providence and guidance.

- In the culture of ancient Israel, shepherds were seen as figures of care and guidance. Shepherding involved leading sheep to safe pastures, protecting them from predators, and ensuring their well-being.
  
    - "How might the image of a shepherd contribute to your understanding of feeling pursued by 'goodness and mercy'?"

**Cultural and Biblical Insights:**
The phrase "goodness and mercy" uses Hebrew words that carry rich meanings. "Goodness" (טוֹב, tov) often implies beneficial, pleasurable states or things, while "mercy" (חֶסֶד, chesed) is a covenantal term signifying loyal love or loving-kindness.

- In a culture where covenants were crucial for societal and relational structures, "chesed" indicated a deep, enduring commitment, often beyond what was legally required.

    - "What does it mean for you personally to think of 'mercy' as a lasting, loyal love in your life?"
    
**Exegetical Lenses:**
Using the covenantal framework, God's promise to David and Israel was one of ongoing faithfulness and committed relationship.

- "How does viewing your life through the lens of a covenant with God alter or deepen your understanding of His presence with you?"

**Imagery and Emotion:**
The promise to "dwell in the house of the Lord forever" reflects a longing for continuous communion with God. In ancient Israel, the house of the Lord was the temple, a central place for encountering God.

- "What emotions are stirred in you when you envision being in the 'house of the Lord' forever?"

**Open-ended Questions:**
- "How might this portrayal of divine presence impact the daily fears and uncertainties David faced as a shepherd and later as a king?"
- "When you think about being followed by 'goodness and mercy,' how might that change the way you walk through challenges or uncertainties?"
